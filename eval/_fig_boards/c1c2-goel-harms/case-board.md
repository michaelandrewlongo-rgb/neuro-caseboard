## Anatomy at Risk
*Structures to identify, preserve, or monitor for this approach.*

- ✓ The surgical corridor is a midline posterior approach from the inion to C3, with subperiosteal dissection exposing the C1 posterior arch and C2 lamina and lateral masses.  (see Fig F1)
  - *Why:* This standard approach provides access to the C1-C2 articulation, but dissection must remain strictly subperiosteal to avoid the vertebral artery laterally and the extensive venous plexus.

- ✓ Key bony landmarks identified in sequence are the external occipital protuberance (inion), the bifid spinous process of C2, and the posterior tubercle of C1 in the midline.  (see Fig F2)
  - *Why:* The C2 spinous process is the primary landmark; from here, dissection proceeds superiorly to C1 and laterally over the lamina to identify the C1-C2 joint.

- ✓ The C2 nerve root and its large dorsal root ganglion emerge between the posterior arch of C1 and the lamina of C2, directly in the path of dissection to the C1-C2 joint.  (see Fig F3)
  - *Why:* This structure must be mobilized or sacrificed to access the joint for decortication and grafting. Mobilization risks hemorrhage from the surrounding venous plexus; sacrifice causes permanent occipital numbness.

- ✓ The spinal cord is at risk for direct injury during screw placement and ischemic injury during reduction of the atlantoaxial subluxation.  (see Fig F4)
  - *Why:* Neuromonitoring is critical to detect cord compromise in real-time, allowing for immediate intervention like releasing the reduction or adjusting screw trajectory.

- ✓ The V3 segment of the vertebral artery (VA) courses through the foramen transversarium of C1 and then runs medially in a groove on the superior surface of the C1 posterior arch.  (see Fig F5)
  - *Why:* Dissection superior to the C1 arch and placement of C1 lateral mass screws carry a high risk of VA injury. The screw trajectory must be planned to avoid this superior course.

- ✓ The C1-C2 epidural venous plexus is a large, fragile network of veins surrounding the C2 nerve root and vertebral artery, and is a source of significant, difficult-to-control bleeding.  (see Fig F6)
  - *Why:* Dissection in this area must be meticulous. Expect significant bleeding and have hemostatic agents (e.g., FloSeal, Surgiflo) and blood products immediately available.

- ✓ Confirm review of the preoperative CTA for a high-riding vertebral artery, where the C2 pars is small and the VA groove is high, increasing risk of injury with C2 pars screw placement.  (see Fig F7)
  - *Why:* This common variant (up to 20% of patients) may necessitate altering the C2 screw from a pars to a pedicle trajectory or using a different fixation technique to avoid catastrophic VA injury.

- ✓ The primary 'no-fly zones' for screw placement are the medial wall of the C1 lateral mass and C2 pedicle (spinal canal) and the superolateral aspect of the C2 pars/pedicle (vertebral artery).
  - *Why:* Breach of these zones can cause spinal cord injury or vertebral artery hemorrhage. Intraoperative navigation and triggered EMG are used to confirm screw placement is within the safe bony corridor.

- ⚠ The hypoglossal nerve (CN XII) can be injured during dissection to the C1-C2 joint if dissection proceeds too far anteriorly, leading to tongue deviation.  (see Fig F8)
  - *Why:* Highlights a key cranial nerve at risk that is not immediately obvious from a posterior approach, defining the anterior limit of safe dissection.

## Operative Plan
*Critical steps, decision points, and stop criteria.*

- ✓ Patient will be positioned prone on a Jackson frame with the head secured in a 3-point Mayfield clamp. The neck will be maintained in a neutral alignment to start, with final adjustments made under fluoroscopy to facilitate reduction.
  - *Why:* Proper positioning is crucial for surgical access, reduction, and preventing pressure sores. Prone positioning also increases risk of venous air embolism.

- ✓ Monitoring
  - *Why:* This provides real-time feedback on spinal cord and nerve root function during critical steps like positioning, reduction, and instrumentation.
  - [ ] Multimodality neuromonitoring will include SSEP, TcMEP, and free-run EMG.
  - [ ] Baseline signals will be obtained after positioning and before incision.
  - [ ] The alert criteria is a >50% decrease in amplitude.

- ✓ The O-arm with Stealth navigation will be used for intraoperative confirmation of screw trajectories. Cell Saver will be set up and ready for potential high-volume blood loss.
  - *Why:* Navigation enhances the safety and accuracy of C1/C2 screw placement, minimizing risk to the spinal cord and vertebral arteries. Cell Saver mitigates the need for allogeneic transfusion.

- ✓ Exposure requires subperiosteal dissection to the lateral border of the C1-C2 facet joints. The C2 nerve root and ganglion will be identified and mobilized inferiorly to expose the joint.
  - *Why:* Inadequate exposure of the lateral mass entry point risks poor screw trajectory. Aggressive mobilization of the C2 ganglion risks hemorrhage from the venous plexus.

- ✓ C1 Lateral Mass Screw Trajectory: Start point is the midpoint of the C1 lateral mass. Trajectory is 10-15 degrees medially convergent, aimed at the anterior tubercle of C1, and parallel to the C1 posterior arch in the sagittal plane.
  - *Why:* This trajectory maximizes screw purchase in the lateral mass while avoiding the spinal cord medially and the vertebral artery superiorly.

- ✓ C2 Pars Screw Trajectory (Goel-Harms): The start point is on the C2 pars, typically 3-4 mm superior to the C2-3 facet. The trajectory is aimed superiorly and medially, parallel to the plane of the pars itself, with approximately 20-25 degrees of medial convergence.
  - *Why:* This clarifies the specific trajectory for a C2 pars screw, which is distinct from a C2 pedicle screw. The original description conflated the two, but the Goel-Harms technique relies on pars fixation. Accuracy is critical to avoid the vertebral artery laterally and the spinal canal medially.

- ✓ Reduction of the atlantoaxial instability will be performed after placing all four screws but before final tightening. The head is gently extended and translated posteriorly while applying distraction with the screw towers.
  - *Why:* This is a high-risk step. Neuromonitoring signals must be stable. The goal is to restore normal alignment of the C1-C2 complex without compressing the spinal cord.

- ✓ The articular surfaces of the C1-C2 joints will be decorticated using a high-speed drill with a diamond burr to expose cancellous bone and promote arthrodesis.
  - *Why:* Solid bony fusion is the ultimate goal for long-term stability. Incomplete decortication is a leading cause of pseudarthrosis (fusion failure).

- ✓ Bone graft (local autograft from C2 spinous process and lamina) will be packed into the decorticated C1-C2 joints and along the C1/C2 lamina after rod placement and final tightening.
  - *Why:* The bone graft provides the osteoinductive and osteoconductive scaffold necessary for a solid C1-C2 fusion.

- ⚠ If the C2 nerve root and ganglion are large and obscure the joint, is the preference to resect the ganglion or spend additional time mobilizing it?
  - *Why:* Resection is faster and provides excellent exposure but guarantees postoperative occipital numbness. Mobilization preserves function but increases operative time and bleeding risk.

- ✓ If an incidental durotomy occurs, it will be repaired primarily with 5-0 Prolene sutures and augmented with a dural sealant. A lumbar drain will not be placed unless the leak is large and unrepairable.
  - *Why:* A watertight dural repair is essential to prevent CSF leak, pseudomeningocele formation, and meningitis. Having a clear, tiered plan avoids intraoperative indecision.

- ✓ Sustained, significant loss of neuromonitoring signals (TcMEPs) that does not respond to increased MAP or position adjustment is a criterion to pause, reverse the last maneuver, and consider aborting the procedure.
  - *Why:* Persistent signal loss indicates impending spinal cord injury. Proceeding risks permanent paralysis.

- ✓ Anesthesia will use a Total Intravenous Anesthesia (TIVA) protocol with Propofol and Remifentanil infusions to avoid inhalational agent suppression of motor evoked potentials (MEPs).
  - *Why:* Ensures reliable neuromonitoring, which is critical for detecting and preventing spinal cord injury during reduction and instrumentation.

- ✓ If the C2 nerve root/ganglion obstructs the C1 lateral mass entry point, it will be sharply divided. The resulting C2 dermatome numbness is an accepted trade-off for safe screw placement and joint preparation.
  - *Why:* Defines the plan for a common intraoperative issue, prioritizing safe hardware placement and robust arthrodesis over preservation of C2 sensation.

- ✓ Patient will be placed in a rigid cervical collar (e.g., Aspen) before leaving the OR and will remain in it at all times until fusion is confirmed on follow-up imaging.
  - *Why:* Provides external stability to protect the internal fixation, reduce stress on the hardware, and promote successful arthrodesis.

## Risk and Rescue
*Expected and catastrophic complications with rescue sequences.*

- ✓ Anesthesia Brief: This is a high-blood-loss case. Use TIVA, maintain MAP >80 mmHg, have 4 units PRBC available, and set up the Cell Saver. Central and arterial lines are required.
  - *Why:* Clear communication with anesthesia ensures readiness for the specific challenges of this case: need for reliable neuromonitoring (TIVA), potential for massive hemorrhage, and risk of VAE.

- ✓ Post-operative C5 palsy (deltoid and/or biceps weakness) can occur days after surgery and must be monitored with frequent motor exams in the ICU.
  - *Why:* This is a known complication of cervical spine surgery, possibly from nerve root traction. Early detection is key, though treatment is primarily supportive.

- ✓ Post-operative dysphagia and airway swelling are significant risks due to prolonged prone positioning and retraction. A formal swallow evaluation will be performed before initiating a diet.
  - *Why:* Failure to recognize dysphagia can lead to aspiration pneumonia. Airway swelling can be life-threatening and may require delayed extubation.

- ✓ Malreduction or poor final alignment can lead to persistent pain, neurologic deficit, or fixation failure. Final lateral fluoroscopy must confirm reduction and a reasonable cervical sagittal alignment.
  - *Why:* The goal is not just fixation, but restoration of stable anatomy. Poor alignment can create a new set of problems.

- ✓ Catastrophic Complications
  - *Why:* This is a life-threatening complication. A clear, calm, step-wise rescue protocol is essential to achieve hemostasis without causing a stroke.
  - [ ] Vertebral Artery Injury.
  - [ ] Signs: pulsatile, bright red bleeding.
  - [ ] Apply direct pressure with cottonoid.
  - [ ] Announce to team.
  - [ ] If bleeding persists, pack area with Surgicel Fibrillar and Gelfoam.
  - [ ] If packing fails, prepare for vessel ligation or endovascular sacrifice post-operatively.
  - [ ] DO NOT blindly clamp.

- ⚠ Catastrophic Complications
  - *Why:* This is the most common source of major blood loss in this surgery. Panicked, rapid attempts at suction or cautery will only worsen it. Patience and packing are key.
  - [ ] Massive Hemorrhage from Venous Plexus.
  - [ ] Signs: voluminous, dark, non-pulsatile bleeding obscuring the field.
  - [ ] Pack firmly with Surgicel and cottonoids.
  - [ ] Apply sustained pressure.
  - [ ] Alert anesthesia to activate Massive Transfusion Protocol.
  - [ ] Work on an alternative area while waiting for hemostasis.

- ⚠ Catastrophic Complications
  - *Why:* VAE can cause cardiovascular collapse. The entire team must be drilled on the immediate, coordinated response to prevent this.
  - [ ] Venous Air Embolism (VAE).
  - [ ] Signs: sudden drop in end-tidal CO2, hypotension, 'mill wheel' murmur.
  - [ ] Surgeon floods the field with irrigation.
  - [ ] Anesthesiologist places patient in Trendelenburg, aspirates from central line, and administers 100% O2.

- ✓ Rescue Triggers
  - *Why:* This protocol provides a systematic approach to troubleshooting potential spinal cord ischemia, prioritizing patient safety over procedural progress.
  - [ ] SSEP/MEP Signal Loss (>50% amplitude drop).
  - [ ] Rescue Protocol: 1.
  - [ ] Announce to team, pause surgery.
  - [ ] Anesthesia: increase MAP to >90mmHg.
  - [ ] Surgeon: release reduction, check head position, remove instruments.
  - [ ] If signals do not return within minutes, consider wake-up test and aborting instrumentation.

- ✓ Post-op trigger for return to OR: Any new or progressive motor deficit, or signs of a compressive hematoma (neck swelling, stridor, dyspnea).
  - *Why:* A post-operative hematoma is a surgical emergency. The rescue is to open the wound at the bedside to decompress the airway, then return to the OR for evacuation.

- ✓ ICU Handoff Plan: Admit to ICU, hard cervical collar at all times, Q1h neuro checks (C5 strength critical), maintain SBP 100-140, monitor drain output, and obtain post-op CT scan in AM to confirm hardware placement.
  - *Why:* A detailed handoff ensures continuity of care and vigilant monitoring for known postoperative complications like hematoma, C5 palsy, and hardware failure.

- ✓ Transfusion will be triggered for Hgb < 8 g/dL or hemodynamic instability responsive to fluids/blood products.
  - *Why:* Establishes a clear, pre-agreed threshold for blood product administration, preventing delays and ensuring team alignment during significant blood loss.

- ✓ Catastrophic Complications
  - *Why:* Provides a clear, step-wise algorithm for managing a potentially lethal complication, emphasizing definitive intraoperative control while accounting for the critical importance of cerebral perfusion.
  - [ ] Vertebral Artery Injury Rescue Sequence: 1.
  - [ ] Immediate tamponade with a cottonoid.
  - [ ] If bleeding continues, pack the foramen transversarium directly with hemostatic agents (e.g., Surgicel Fibrillar, bone wax).
  - [ ] If uncontrolled, prepare for definitive vessel sacrifice by packing or ligation, but only after confirming contralateral VA patency on pre-op imaging.
  - [ ] If contralateral VA is absent or atretic, abort sacrifice and consider emergent angiography.
