## Anatomy at Risk
*Structures to identify, preserve, or monitor for this approach.*

- ✓ The surgical corridor is the right Sylvian fissure. The split will be performed 'outside-in,' starting laterally over the pars triangularis and moving medially to expose the M1 segment.  (see Fig F1)
  - *Why:* This approach minimizes retraction on the frontal and temporal lobes and uses natural arachnoid planes to reach the deep vasculature, but requires careful handling of the superficial Sylvian veins.

- ✓ After drilling the sphenoid ridge, identify the optic nerve and supraclinoid ICA. Follow the ICA distally to its bifurcation, then trace the M1 segment laterally into the Sylvian fissure to find the aneurysm.  (see Fig F2)
  - *Why:* This sequence provides a reliable roadmap to the aneurysm. Losing this sequence risks disorientation and injury to critical structures.

- ✓ The right optic nerve (CN II) lies directly inferior and medial to the supraclinoid ICA and M1 origin. It is at high risk from fixed retractors and thermal injury from the drill or bipolar.  (see Fig F3)
  - *Why:* Injury to the optic nerve is catastrophic and irreversible, leading to monocular blindness. It must be visually identified and protected throughout the exposure.

- ⚠ The oculomotor nerve (CN III) is located in the oculomotor triangle, inferior to the aneurysm complex, and can be adherent to the dome if it projects inferiorly.  (see Fig F4)
  - *Why:* Injury to CN III results in a fixed, dilated pupil and ptosis with impaired eye movement ('down and out' gaze), which can be confused with uncal herniation post-operatively.

- ⚠ The lenticulostriate arteries arise from the superior and posterior surface of the M1 segment and are frequently adherent to the aneurysm dome or neck.  (see Fig F5)
  - *Why:* Occlusion of these small perforators causes stroke in the basal ganglia and internal capsule, leading to significant motor deficits (hemiparesis).

- ⚠ The superficial Sylvian veins within the fissure must be preserved if possible. Major draining veins are dissected free, while minor bridging veins may be coagulated and divided to deepen the corridor.  (see Fig F6)
  - *Why:* Sacrifice of a dominant Sylvian vein can lead to venous congestion, swelling, and hemorrhagic infarction of the temporal or frontal lobe.

- ⚠ The right superior temporal gyrus, forming the inferior bank of the Sylvian fissure, is at risk for retraction injury, potentially causing subtle auditory or memory deficits.  (see Fig F7)
  - *Why:* Even on the non-dominant side, injury to the temporal lobe can have consequences for cognitive function and quality of life.

- ✓ Confirm the M1 bifurcation anatomy on the angiogram. An 'early' bifurcation (<1 cm from ICA) shortens the M1 segment, making proximal control with a temporary clip more challenging.  (see Fig F8)
  - *Why:* Anatomic variations dictate clipping strategy. A short M1 may require temporary clipping of the ICA, which carries a higher risk of ischemia.

- ✓ The medial and posterior aspects of the aneurysm neck are a 'no-fly zone' for dissection until the dome is mobilized and perforators are visualized, as this is where lenticulostriates are most often adherent.
  - *Why:* Blind dissection in this area is the most common cause of perforator injury. Dissect from parent vessel towards the aneurysm neck.

- ⚠ The anterior temporal artery (ATA) is an early branch of the M1 segment and serves as a key landmark during the Sylvian fissure split, helping to orient the surgeon to the M1 trunk.
  - *Why:* Identifying the ATA early in the dissection confirms the location of the M1 segment, allowing for a more confident and efficient dissection towards the aneurysm neck.

- ✓ The recurrent artery of Heubner, typically arising from A1 or A2 near the ACOM, supplies the anterior limb of the internal capsule. It can be near the ICA bifurcation and must be identified and preserved.
  - *Why:* Injury to Heubner's artery can cause a contralateral facial and arm weakness (brachiocervical > leg) and aphasia (if dominant), a devastating stroke that must be avoided.

## Operative Plan
*Critical steps, decision points, and stop criteria.*

- ⚠ Patient is supine, head in a Mayfield holder, rotated 30-45 degrees to the left, with chin tucked and vertex tilted towards the floor. This makes the pterion the highest point in the surgical field.
  - *Why:* This 'head-down' position uses gravity to help the frontal lobe fall away from the skull base, reducing the need for fixed retraction and minimizing retraction injury.

- ⚠ The head of bed will be elevated 15-20 degrees. This improves venous drainage but places the dural sinuses above the heart, creating a risk for venous air embolism (VAE).
  - *Why:* Anesthesia must be prepared to detect and manage VAE, which can cause hypotension and cardiac arrest. A precordial Doppler and end-tidal CO2 monitoring are essential.

- ✓ A pterional craniotomy will be performed, followed by aggressive drilling of the sphenoid wing until it is flush with the floor of the anterior and middle fossae.
  - *Why:* Flattening the sphenoid ridge provides a direct, unobstructed view of the carotid cistern and Sylvian fissure, which is critical for minimizing brain retraction.

- ✓ If not already in place, a right frontal external ventricular drain (EVD) will be placed after the craniotomy flap is elevated to assist with brain relaxation and monitor post-operative ICP.
  - *Why:* In a HH3 patient with a swollen brain, CSF drainage via an EVD is essential for achieving a slack brain, which is a prerequisite for safe microsurgical dissection.

- ⚠ Brain relaxation will be achieved with Mannitol (1 g/kg) at skin incision and controlled drainage of CSF from the basal cisterns (opticocarotid and chiasmatic) upon dural opening.
  - *Why:* A tense, swollen brain makes every step of the operation more difficult and dangerous, increasing the risk of retraction injury and intraoperative rupture.

- ✓ Before final neck dissection, we will achieve proximal control by dissecting a 1 cm segment of the M1 proximal to the aneurysm. A temporary clip will be on the instrument table, ready for immediate deployment.
  - *Why:* Having proximal control is the primary safety measure against catastrophic intraoperative rupture. Without it, hemorrhage can be uncontrollable.

- ✓ The final clip must completely obliterate the aneurysm neck while preserving the origins of both M2 branches. The clip blades should be parallel to the M2 trunks.
  - *Why:* Improper clip placement is the primary cause of surgical failure, leading to either re-bleeding (incomplete closure) or stroke (parent vessel stenosis/occlusion).

- ✓ A temporary clip will be applied to the proximal M1 if the aneurysm ruptures, or electively to soften the dome for safer dissection. Each application should not exceed 5 minutes.
  - *Why:* Temporary clipping facilitates control and dissection but carries a time-dependent risk of ischemia. The duration must be tracked and minimized.

- ✓ If an intraoperative rupture cannot be controlled with suction and temporary clipping, we will induce transient cardiac arrest with a 6-12 mg bolus of Adenosine.
  - *Why:* Adenosine provides ~30 seconds of flow arrest, creating a bloodless field for precise clip placement. Anesthesia must be ready to administer it on command.

- ⚠ Dural closure must be watertight, using primary sutures supplemented with a dural onlay graft (e.g., DuraGen) and sealant (e.g., DuraSeal) to prevent postoperative CSF leak.
  - *Why:* A CSF leak increases the risk of meningitis, pseudomeningocele formation, and wound breakdown, requiring re-operation.

- ✓ After final clip placement, we will use ICG videoangiography and a micro-Doppler probe to confirm complete aneurysm obliteration and patency of the M1 and both M2 branches.
  - *Why:* Visual inspection alone is unreliable. These adjuncts provide objective evidence of successful clipping and prevent 'closing the head' on an occluded vessel or residual aneurysm.

- ⚠ We will use somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEPs) and motor evoked potentials (MEPs). A sustained amplitude drop of >50% after clip placement is a trigger to immediately inspect and reposition the clip.
  - *Why:* Neuromonitoring provides real-time feedback on the functional integrity of neural pathways at risk of ischemia, allowing for intraoperative correction before a deficit becomes permanent.

- ✓ The anesthesia team must have 2 units of cross-matched packed red blood cells in the operating room, and adenosine drawn up and ready before the dura is opened.
  - *Why:* Preparation is key. An intraoperative rupture can lead to massive, rapid blood loss. Delays in obtaining blood or rescue medications can be fatal.

- ⚠ Confirm the pre-clipping systolic blood pressure target is <140 mmHg. Post-clipping, the target will be raised to a range of 140-180 mmHg to augment perfusion and mitigate vasospasm.
  - *Why:* Blood pressure management is a balancing act: too high pre-clipping increases rupture risk; too low post-clipping increases ischemia risk from vasospasm.

- ⚠ Will we obtain a routine post-operative CTA within 24 hours to document clip position and vessel patency, or will we rely on intra-operative findings and only get a CTA for a clinical change?
  - *Why:* A baseline post-op vascular study provides definitive documentation of the surgical result and is invaluable for interpreting future imaging if the patient's condition changes.

- ✓ After dural opening and before significant retraction, the arachnoid over the opticocarotid cistern will be opened sharply to release CSF and achieve initial brain relaxation.
  - *Why:* This is the first and most important step for creating space. It reduces the need for fixed retraction on the frontal lobe, minimizing the risk of contusion and edema.

- ⚠ For further brain relaxation in a tense brain (HH3), the lamina terminalis will be fenestrated between the optic chiasm and ACOM to release CSF directly from the third ventricle.
  - *Why:* In a swollen brain from SAH, cisternal CSF may be insufficient. Fenestrating the lamina terminalis provides profound brain relaxation, improving exposure and reducing retraction injury.

- ✓ A full set of permanent and temporary aneurysm clips (straight, curved, angled, fenestrated; multiple lengths and blade types) must be available on the field.
  - *Why:* Aneurysm neck geometry is variable. Having a comprehensive clip set immediately available is essential for selecting the optimal clip to ensure complete obliteration without compromising parent or perforating vessels.

- ✓ Confirm the post-operative hydrocephalus management plan: Wean EVD if ICPs are <15 mmHg with clamping trials starting post-op day 3-5. Proceed to ventriculoperitoneal shunt if weaning fails.
  - *Why:* SAH patients have a high rate of chronic hydrocephalus. A pre-defined plan for EVD weaning and shunt triggers ensures timely management and avoids prolonged ICU stays or neurological decline.

## Risk and Rescue
*Expected and catastrophic complications with rescue sequences.*

- ✓ Ischemic stroke from clip-related stenosis of the M1/M2 origins or avulsion of a lenticulostriate perforator is the most common, feared complication of this procedure.
  - *Why:* This results in a permanent neurological deficit (hemiparesis, aphasia). Meticulous dissection and confirmation with ICG/Doppler are the primary mitigation strategies.

- ✓ Delayed cerebral ischemia (DCI) from post-SAH vasospasm is a major risk, typically peaking on post-bleed days 7-10. This patient's HH3 grade puts them at high risk.
  - *Why:* Vasospasm can cause strokes in territories remote from the surgery. It requires vigilant monitoring and aggressive management with induced hypertension and possible angioplasty.

- ✓ Post-operative seizures are common after SAH and craniotomy due to cortical irritation from blood products and surgical manipulation.
  - *Why:* Seizures increase metabolic demand and can worsen secondary brain injury. Prophylactic anti-epileptic drugs are standard of care.

- ✓ Catastrophic Complications
  - *Why:* This is a high-stress, time-critical emergency. The team must be prepared to execute this sequence calmly and efficiently to prevent exsanguination or irreversible brain injury.
  - [ ] Intra-operative aneurysm rupture.
  - [ ] Rescue sequence: 1) Announce 'RUPTURE'.
  - [ ] 2) Apply large-bore suction directly to the bleeding point to clear the field (sucker-decompression).
  - [ ] 3) Apply the temporary clip to the proximal M1.
  - [ ] 4) Once controlled, proceed with definitive clipping.

- ✓ Catastrophic Complications
  - *Why:* A major stroke is devastating. Early recognition and intervention (repositioning clip, decompressive craniectomy) are the only ways to mitigate the outcome.
  - [ ] Malignant MCA infarction from parent vessel occlusion.
  - [ ] Rescue: If ICG/Doppler shows M1/M2 occlusion, the clip must be immediately repositioned.
  - [ ] If a new major deficit is found on wake-up, a STAT CT/CTA is performed.
  - [ ] If malignant edema develops, proceed to decompressive hemicraniectomy.

- ✓ Catastrophic Complications
  - *Why:* Post-operative re-rupture has an extremely high mortality rate. There is no time for delay; this requires immediate diagnosis and surgical re-intervention.
  - [ ] Acute post-operative re-bleeding from a slipped clip.
  - [ ] Rescue: A sudden neurological decline (e.g., blown pupil, GCS drop) triggers a STAT non-contrast head CT.
  - [ ] If a large hematoma is confirmed, the patient is taken for emergent return to the OR for evacuation and re-clipping.

- ⚠ To prevent vasospasm, the patient will receive Nimodipine 60 mg every 4 hours (PO or NG) for 21 days, starting immediately. Maintain euvolemia and normal electrolytes.
  - *Why:* Nimodipine is the only drug proven to improve outcomes by reducing the incidence of ischemic deficits from vasospasm after SAH.

- ✓ Seizure prophylaxis with Levetiracetam 1000 mg IV/PO BID will be initiated pre-operatively and continued for at least 7 days post-op to prevent seizures from SAH-induced cortical irritability.
  - *Why:* Preventing seizures avoids secondary insults like increased ICP, metabolic demand, and potential aspiration that can worsen the patient's outcome.

- ✓ Rescue Triggers
  - *Why:* A structured, rapid response to neurological change is critical to differentiating between hemorrhage, hydrocephalus, seizure, and ischemia, each of which requires a different and urgent treatment.
  - [ ] Any post-operative neurological decline (GCS drop >2, new focal deficit, pupillary change) triggers the 'Post-op Decline' protocol: 1) STAT non-contrast head CT.
  - [ ] 2) Check EVD function.
  - [ ] 3) If CT shows hemorrhage, return to OR.
  - [ ] 4) If CT is negative, obtain CTA/CTP to assess for vasospasm.

- ✓ A sustained ICP reading > 20 mmHg for more than 5 minutes, unresponsive to EVD drainage, is a trigger for escalation: bolus of 3% saline or mannitol, ensure adequate sedation, and prepare for possible decompressive hemicraniectomy if medical management fails.
  - *Why:* Uncontrolled intracranial hypertension causes secondary brain injury and can lead to herniation and death. It requires a stepwise and aggressive management algorithm.

- ✓ During temporary M1 occlusion, neuroprotection will be provided by inducing burst suppression with an IV bolus of propofol or etomidate to decrease cerebral metabolic demand (CMRO2).
  - *Why:* Reducing the brain's metabolic needs provides a margin of safety against ischemia during temporary clipping, especially if prolonged or repeated applications are necessary.

- ✓ Rescue Triggers
  - *Why:* This pre-draping 'closed-loop' communication ensures the entire team is aligned on critical hemodynamic goals, drug availability, and triggers for intraoperative alerts, preventing errors and delays.
  - [ ] Anesthesia Team Brief: Maintain SBP <140 mmHg until 'aneurysm secured' is announced.
  - [ ] Have adenosine (6mg, 12mg) and cross-matched blood in the room.
  - [ ] Be prepared for induced hypertension (SBP 140-180) post-clipping.
  - [ ] Announce any SSEP/MEP changes >50% immediately.
