## Anatomy at Risk
*Structures to identify, preserve, or monitor for this approach.*

- ⚠ The surgical corridor for a C5-6 ACDF is through the anterior neck, typically between the sternocleidomastoid and the trachea/esophagus.
  - *Why:* Proper identification of the surgical corridor minimizes the risk of injury to surrounding structures such as the carotid artery and esophagus.

- ⚠ Key landmarks in order for a C5-6 ACDF include the hyoid bone, thyroid cartilage, and the carotid tubercle at C6.
  - *Why:* Accurate identification of these landmarks ensures correct level localization and minimizes the risk of wrong-level surgery.

- ⚠ The recurrent laryngeal nerve runs in the tracheoesophageal groove; the right-sided course is more variable and at risk during dissection.
  - *Why:* Injury to the recurrent laryngeal nerve can result in vocal cord paralysis, leading to hoarseness or airway compromise.

- ⚠ The carotid artery is located laterally and must be carefully retracted to avoid injury during a C5-6 ACDF.
  - *Why:* Inadvertent injury to the carotid artery can lead to catastrophic bleeding and stroke.

- ⚠ The esophagus lies medially and must be protected during retraction to prevent postoperative dysphagia.
  - *Why:* Esophageal injury can lead to serious complications such as infection or fistula formation.

- ⚠ The vertebral artery may have an anomalous course and should be identified on pre-operative imaging.
  - *Why:* Anomalous vertebral artery anatomy can increase the risk of vascular injury during discectomy.

- ⚠ The area posterior to the posterior longitudinal ligament is a no-fly zone to avoid spinal cord injury.
  - *Why:* Entering this area can result in direct trauma to the spinal cord, causing neurological deficits.

## Operative Plan
*Critical steps, decision points, and stop criteria.*

- ⚠ The patient is positioned supine with slight neck extension and the head turned slightly to the left for a right-sided approach.
  - *Why:* Proper positioning provides optimal exposure and minimizes tension on the neural structures.

- ⚠ Exposure involves a transverse incision along a skin crease, followed by dissection through the platysma and identification of the prevertebral fascia.
  - *Why:* Adequate exposure is crucial for safe access to the cervical spine and minimizes soft tissue trauma.

- ⚠ Critical steps include discectomy, endplate preparation, and placement of the interbody graft and anterior plate.
  - *Why:* Each step must be performed meticulously to ensure spinal stability and successful decompression.

- ⚠ Decision points include the choice of graft material and whether to use a standalone cage or an additional anterior plate.
  - *Why:* These decisions impact the biomechanical stability and fusion rate of the construct.

- ⚠ Stop criteria include achieving adequate decompression of the neural elements and stable construct placement.
  - *Why:* Ensuring these criteria are met reduces the risk of postoperative complications and need for revision surgery.

- ⚠ Closure involves re-approximation of the platysma and subcutaneous tissues, followed by skin closure with absorbable sutures.
  - *Why:* Proper closure technique reduces the risk of wound complications and promotes healing.

- ⚠ Intraoperative neuromonitoring of somatosensory and motor evoked potentials is used to detect potential neural compromise.
  - *Why:* Monitoring helps prevent intraoperative neurological injury by providing real-time feedback on neural function.

- ⚠ A high-speed drill is used for osteophyte removal and endplate preparation during the procedure.
  - *Why:* The drill facilitates precise bone removal and preparation for graft placement.

## Risk and Rescue
*Expected and catastrophic complications with rescue sequences.*

- ⚠ Likely complications include dysphagia, hoarseness, and graft subsidence.
  - *Why:* Awareness of these complications allows for early intervention and management to improve patient outcomes.

- ⚠ Catastrophic complications include esophageal perforation and carotid artery injury.
  - *Why:* These complications require immediate recognition and intervention to prevent life-threatening consequences.

- ⚠ Mitigation strategies include careful retraction techniques and preoperative imaging review to identify anatomical variants.
  - *Why:* These strategies reduce the risk of intraoperative injury to critical structures.

- ⚠ Rescue triggers include sudden loss of neuromonitoring signals or unexpected bleeding, prompting immediate cessation of the procedure and assessment.
  - *Why:* Timely recognition and response to these triggers can prevent permanent neurological deficits or catastrophic hemorrhage.
