## Anatomy at Risk
*Structures to identify, preserve, or monitor for this approach.*

- ⚠ The surgical corridor for C5-6 ACDF involves a transverse incision along the anterior neck, typically at the level of the C5-6 interspace, with dissection through the platysma and medial retraction of the sternocleidomastoid muscle.
  - *Why:* Proper identification and creation of the surgical corridor is crucial to minimize damage to surrounding structures and ensure adequate exposure of the cervical spine.

- ⚠ Key landmarks in order for C5-6 ACDF include the hyoid bone, thyroid cartilage, and carotid tubercle (Chassaignac's tubercle) which is located at the level of C6.
  - *Why:* Accurate identification of these landmarks helps in confirming the correct level for incision and approach, reducing the risk of operating at the wrong spinal level.

- ⚠ The recurrent laryngeal nerve runs in the tracheoesophageal groove, with the right-sided course being more variable, and is at risk during retraction of the trachea and esophagus.
  - *Why:* Injury to the recurrent laryngeal nerve can result in vocal cord paralysis, leading to hoarseness or airway compromise.

- ⚠ The vertebral artery runs laterally to the cervical vertebrae and is at risk if the dissection extends too far laterally.
  - *Why:* Injury to the vertebral artery can lead to significant hemorrhage and potential stroke.

- ⚠ The esophagus lies anterior to the cervical spine and must be carefully retracted to avoid injury during exposure.
  - *Why:* Esophageal injury can lead to fistula formation and mediastinitis, which are serious complications.

- ⚠ Anatomical variants such as a high-riding vertebral artery or an aberrant thyroid artery can alter the surgical approach and increase risk.
  - *Why:* Awareness of these variants is essential to avoid vascular injury during the procedure.

- ⚠ The carotid sheath, containing the carotid artery, internal jugular vein, and vagus nerve, is a no-fly zone and must be avoided during dissection.
  - *Why:* Injury to the carotid sheath structures can result in catastrophic hemorrhage or nerve damage.

- ⚠ Which specific feeding vessels to the vertebral artery should be identified and preserved during C5-6 ACDF?
  - *Why:* Identifying and preserving specific feeding vessels to the vertebral artery is crucial to prevent ischemic complications and ensure adequate blood supply during the procedure.

## Operative Plan
*Critical steps, decision points, and stop criteria.*

- ⚠ Position the patient supine with the neck in slight extension, using a shoulder roll to optimize exposure of the cervical spine.
  - *Why:* Proper positioning is critical to ensure adequate surgical access and minimize risk of pressure-related injuries.

- ⚠ Exposure involves a transverse incision, dissection through the platysma, and retraction of the sternocleidomastoid muscle to access the cervical spine.
  - *Why:* Adequate exposure is essential to perform the procedure safely and effectively, minimizing the risk of injury to surrounding structures.

- ⚠ Critical steps include identification of the correct disc space, discectomy, preparation of endplates, and placement of the interbody graft and plate.
  - *Why:* Each step must be executed precisely to ensure successful decompression and stabilization of the cervical spine.

- ⚠ Decision points include choosing the type of interbody graft (e.g., PEEK cage, allograft) and whether to use anterior plating.
  - *Why:* These decisions impact the stability and fusion rate, influencing the overall outcome of the surgery.

- ⚠ Stop the procedure if there is uncontrollable bleeding from the vertebral artery or if the correct level cannot be identified.
  - *Why:* Recognizing when to stop can prevent further complications and allows for reassessment of the surgical plan.

- ⚠ Closure involves careful reapproximation of the platysma and skin, ensuring no tension on the wound to promote healing.
  - *Why:* Proper closure reduces the risk of wound dehiscence and infection.

- ⚠ Intraoperative neuromonitoring, including somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEPs) and electromyography (EMG), is used to monitor neural integrity.
  - *Why:* Monitoring helps detect and prevent neural injury during the procedure.

- ⚠ Use of fluoroscopy is critical for confirming the correct level and placement of hardware.
  - *Why:* Fluoroscopy ensures accurate localization and reduces the risk of operating at the wrong level.

- ⚠ Preference for interbody graft material and plating system should be confirmed with the attending prior to the procedure.
  - *Why:* Aligning with the attending's preferences ensures consistency in surgical technique and outcomes.

- ⚠ What role does intraoperative micro-Doppler play during C5-6 ACDF?
  - *Why:* Intraoperative micro-Doppler can help assess the patency and flow within the vertebral artery, reducing the risk of inadvertent injury during lateral dissection.

## Risk and Rescue
*Expected and catastrophic complications with rescue sequences.*

- ⚠ Dysphagia is a common complication post-ACDF due to esophageal retraction.
  - *Why:* Understanding this risk allows for preoperative counseling and postoperative management to mitigate patient discomfort.

- ⚠ C5 palsy can occur postoperatively, characterized by deltoid weakness.
  - *Why:* Early recognition and management can improve patient outcomes and reduce long-term deficits.

- ⚠ Adjacent segment disease may develop over time, leading to further degeneration above or below the fused segment.
  - *Why:* Awareness of this complication is important for long-term follow-up and patient counseling.

- ⚠ Hardware failure, such as screw loosening or plate fracture, can occur and may require revision surgery.
  - *Why:* Monitoring for hardware integrity is crucial during follow-up to prevent instability.

- ⚠ Infection, although rare, can occur and may necessitate debridement and antibiotic therapy.
  - *Why:* Early detection and treatment of infection are vital to prevent further complications.

- ⚠ Esophageal perforation is a catastrophic complication that requires immediate recognition and repair.
  - *Why:* Delay in addressing an esophageal perforation can lead to mediastinitis and sepsis.

- ⚠ For an expanding neck hematoma causing airway compromise, immediately open the wound at bedside, evacuate the clot, and secure the airway.
  - *Why:* Rapid intervention is critical to prevent airway obstruction and ensure patient safety.

- ⚠ Vertebral artery injury can lead to massive hemorrhage and stroke.
  - *Why:* Prompt control of bleeding and possible endovascular intervention are necessary to manage this complication.

- ⚠ In the event of vertebral artery injury, pack the area with hemostatic agents and consider endovascular consultation.
  - *Why:* Immediate action is required to control bleeding and prevent neurological sequelae.

- ⚠ Spinal cord injury during the procedure can result in paralysis.
  - *Why:* Preventive measures and immediate recognition are essential to minimize permanent damage.

- ⚠ If spinal cord injury is suspected, halt the procedure, assess with intraoperative neuromonitoring, and consider imaging to evaluate the extent of injury.
  - *Why:* Timely intervention can mitigate further damage and improve outcomes.
