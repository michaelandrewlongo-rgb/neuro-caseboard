## Anatomy at Risk
*Structures to identify, preserve, or monitor for this approach.*

- ⚠ The surgical corridor for a C5-6 ACDF involves a left-sided approach to protect the recurrent laryngeal nerve, which is more consistently located on the left side within the tracheoesophageal groove.
  - *Why:* Choosing the left-sided approach minimizes the risk of recurrent laryngeal nerve injury, which can lead to vocal cord paralysis and hoarseness.

- ⚠ The key landmarks in order for the C5-6 ACDF include the skin incision, platysma, sternocleidomastoid muscle, carotid sheath, and longus colli muscles.
  - *Why:* Accurate identification and sequential dissection through these landmarks ensure safe access to the cervical spine while avoiding injury to critical structures.

- ⚠ The C5 nerve root is at risk during the procedure, particularly due to its proximity to the surgical field and potential for traction injury.
  - *Why:* C5 nerve root palsy can result in deltoid weakness, significantly impacting shoulder function and patient quality of life.

- ⚠ The vertebral artery serves as the lateral limit of safe dissection during the procedure, especially during uncinate process resection.
  - *Why:* Injury to the vertebral artery can lead to catastrophic bleeding and potential stroke, necessitating careful dissection to avoid this structure.

- ⚠ The posterior longitudinal ligament resection is critical for adequate central decompression to relieve myelopathy.
  - *Why:* Failure to adequately decompress the spinal cord can result in persistent myelopathy symptoms, affecting the patient's neurological function.

- ⚠ Anatomical variants such as a high-riding vertebral artery or aberrant course of the recurrent laryngeal nerve should be identified pre-operatively using imaging studies.
  - *Why:* Recognizing anatomical variants pre-operatively helps in planning the surgical approach and avoiding inadvertent injury to these structures.

- ⚠ The surgical corridor is developed in the avascular plane medial to the carotid sheath and lateral to the tracheoesophageal structures; the carotid sheath itself is mobilized and retracted laterally, not dissected through.
  - *Why:* This clarifies the relationship between the surgical corridor and the key retracted structures, preventing injury to the carotid artery, jugular vein, and vagus nerve within the sheath.

- ⚠ The cervical sympathetic chain lies on the anterior surface of the longus colli muscle, lateral to the dissection plane. To prevent Horner's syndrome, dissection and retractor placement must remain medial to or on the muscle belly, avoiding lateral stripping.
  - *Why:* Injury to the sympathetic chain causes ipsilateral ptosis, miosis, and anhidrosis (Horner's syndrome), a permanent and noticeable complication.

## Operative Plan
*Critical steps, decision points, and stop criteria.*

- ⚠ The patient is positioned supine with the head in slight extension and the shoulders taped down to optimize access to the cervical spine.
  - *Why:* Proper positioning is crucial to ensure adequate exposure of the surgical site while minimizing the risk of pressure ulcers and venous air embolism.

- ⚠ Exposure involves a transverse incision along the skin crease, dissection through the platysma, and retraction of the sternocleidomastoid and carotid sheath to access the cervical spine.
  - *Why:* Adequate exposure is necessary to perform the procedure safely and effectively, minimizing the risk of injury to surrounding structures.

- ⚠ Critical steps include discectomy, decompression of the right C6 nerve root, and placement of the interbody cage followed by anterior plating.
  - *Why:* Each step is essential for achieving the goals of decompression and stabilization, directly impacting the surgical outcome and patient recovery.

- ⚠ Decision points include determining the adequacy of decompression and the size and type of interbody cage to restore segmental lordosis.
  - *Why:* These decisions affect the immediate and long-term success of the surgery, influencing both neurological outcomes and spinal alignment.

- ⚠ The procedure should be stopped if there is uncontrollable bleeding from the vertebral artery or if there is a significant dural tear with CSF leak requiring repair.
  - *Why:* Recognizing when to stop and address complications is crucial to prevent worsening of the patient's condition and to plan for appropriate intervention.

- ⚠ Closure involves meticulous hemostasis, re-approximation of the platysma, and subcuticular skin closure to minimize scarring and infection risk.
  - *Why:* Proper closure techniques reduce the risk of post-operative complications such as hematoma, infection, and poor cosmetic outcomes.

- ⚠ Intraoperative neuromonitoring with SSEP and MEP is used to assess spinal cord and nerve root function during the procedure.
  - *Why:* Neuromonitoring helps detect potential neurological compromise early, allowing for immediate corrective action to prevent permanent injury.

- ⚠ Intraoperative fluoroscopy is used for accurate level localization and confirmation of hardware placement.
  - *Why:* Fluoroscopy ensures that the correct spinal level is addressed and that implants are positioned accurately, reducing the risk of revision surgery.

- ⚠ Verify the attending's preference for interbody cage material and plating system to be used in the procedure.
  - *Why:* Understanding the attending's preferences ensures the surgical team is prepared with the appropriate equipment, facilitating a smooth operation.

- ⚠ The longus colli muscles are elevated from the vertebral bodies starting medially and dissecting laterally, creating muscle flaps that protect the sympathetic chain and can be loosely reapproximated over the plate at closure.
  - *Why:* This technique defines the lateral exposure limits, protects the sympathetic chain, and may reduce the risk of post-operative hematoma by covering the plate.

- ⚠ Hemostasis for epidural venous bleeding is achieved with bipolar cautery and hemostatic agents like Gelfoam/Surgifoam soaked in thrombin. Bleeding from vertebral body endplates is controlled with bone wax.
  - *Why:* Effective, layer-specific hemostasis is critical to maintain visualization during decompression and to prevent post-operative hematoma.

- ⚠ Anterior plate screws should be directed with slight medial and convergent/divergent angulation to achieve bicortical purchase while avoiding the spinal canal, vertebral artery, and adjacent disc spaces.
  - *Why:* Proper screw trajectory is essential for implant stability and preventing catastrophic neurovascular injury or damage to adjacent levels.

- ⚠ Anesthesia brief: Request controlled hypotension (MAP 60-70 mmHg) to minimize epidural bleeding, and a single dose of dexamethasone (e.g., 10mg IV) prior to incision to reduce post-operative soft tissue swelling and dysphagia.
  - *Why:* Proactive communication with anesthesia optimizes the surgical field and can mitigate common post-operative complications like airway swelling and difficulty swallowing.

- ⚠ Immediate post-operative watch includes hourly neuro checks for 4-6 hours, frequent monitoring for neck swelling or stridor (hematoma), and a formal swallow evaluation before advancing diet beyond clear liquids.
  - *Why:* A strict post-operative monitoring protocol ensures early detection of devastating complications like epidural hematoma causing paralysis or neck hematoma causing airway loss.

## Risk and Rescue
*Expected and catastrophic complications with rescue sequences.*

- ⚠ Post-operative dysphagia is a likely complication due to esophageal retraction and manipulation during the procedure.
  - *Why:* Dysphagia can impact the patient's ability to eat and drink, requiring monitoring and potentially dietary modifications or speech therapy.

- ⚠ Vertebral artery injury can lead to massive hemorrhage and stroke; immediate packing and hemostasis followed by vascular repair or embolization is required.
  - *Why:* Timely recognition and management of vertebral artery injury are critical to prevent life-threatening bleeding and neurological deficits.

- ⚠ Mitigation of vertebral artery injury relies on careful review of pre-operative CTA/MRA to identify its course, followed by meticulous uncinatectomy performed with a small kerrison rongeur or burr under direct vision.
  - *Why:* This emphasizes standard surgical technique and anatomical knowledge over reliance on advanced technology which is not routinely used for this procedure.

- ⚠ An expanding neck hematoma post-operatively requires immediate bedside evacuation to prevent airway compromise, followed by return to the OR for definitive management.
  - *Why:* Rapid intervention is necessary to secure the airway and prevent respiratory distress, which can be life-threatening if not addressed promptly.

- ⚠ Carotid sinus reflex (bradycardia/hypotension) is triggered by retractor pressure on the carotid bifurcation (C3-4 level); immediate management is to release the retractor and have anesthesia administer atropine or glycopyrrolate if needed.
  - *Why:* The surgeon must recognize this reflex as the cause of sudden hemodynamic instability and direct the immediate, simple intervention: release the retractor.

- ⚠ Catastrophic Complications
  - *Why:* Having a pre-rehearsed, step-wise algorithm for this high-stakes complication is critical to prevent exsanguination or stroke.
  - [ ] Vertebral artery injury rescue: 1.
  - [ ] Pack with hemostatic agent (e.g., Surgicel, Gelfoam).
  - [ ] Apply direct pressure.
  - [ ] If bleeding persists, obtain proximal/distal control with aneurysm clips.
  - [ ] If control fails, abandon ACDF and proceed to angiography for stenting or embolization.

- ⚠ Esophageal perforation is recognized by saliva or food matter in the wound; repair involves a 2-layer closure (mucosa and muscularis), extensive irrigation, drain placement, and consultation with a thoracic or general surgeon. Patient will be NPO with feeding tube post-op.
  - *Why:* A missed esophageal injury can lead to deep neck space infection, mediastinitis, and sepsis, which are often fatal. Intra-operative recognition and formal repair are critical.

- ⚠ Minimize retraction pressure on the esophagus and carotid sheath; periodically release self-retaining retractors (e.g., every 30-60 minutes) to allow for tissue reperfusion and confirm cuff pressure is <30 mmHg if using an inflatable retractor.
  - *Why:* Sustained, high-pressure retraction is a primary cause of post-operative dysphagia, hoarseness, and carotid injury. Intermittent release mitigates ischemic injury.

- ⚠ Graft subsidence risk is mitigated by meticulous endplate preparation (removing cartilage but preserving subchondral bone), avoiding over-distraction, and selecting an appropriately sized graft that contacts the cortical rim of the vertebral bodies.
  - *Why:* Proper endplate preparation and graft sizing are key to preventing loss of correction, foraminal stenosis recurrence, and hardware failure.

- ⚠ Post-operative dysphagia is managed with a short course of steroids, a soft diet, and speech pathology consultation for a formal swallow study if symptoms are severe or prolonged beyond 2-3 days.
  - *Why:* Having a clear management plan for this common complication improves patient comfort and prevents aspiration-related morbidity.

- ⚠ Rescue Triggers
  - *Why:* A watertight dural closure is necessary to prevent CSF fistula, pseudomeningocele, and meningitis. A clear plan is needed as primary repair is often not feasible.
  - [ ] Incidental durotomy repair: 1.
  - [ ] Attempt primary 4-0 or 5-0 Prolene repair if possible.
  - [ ] If not possible, apply an onlay patch (e.g., DuraGen) and fibrin sealant (e.g., Tisseel).
  - [ ] Consider placing a lumbar drain post-operatively for 2-3 days, especially for larger tears.
