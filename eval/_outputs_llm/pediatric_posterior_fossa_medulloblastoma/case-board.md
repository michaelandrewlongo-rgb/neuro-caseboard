## Anatomy at Risk
*Structures to identify, preserve, or monitor for this approach.*

- ⚠ The telovelar approach is preferred over the transvermian approach to reduce the risk of cerebellar mutism in suboccipital craniotomy for fourth-ventricle medulloblastoma resection.
  - *Why:* The telovelar approach avoids splitting the vermis, which is associated with a lower risk of postoperative cerebellar mutism, a significant complication affecting speech and coordination.

- ⚠ The surgical corridor involves identifying the cerebellar tonsils, uvula, and the tela choroidea to access the fourth ventricle.
  - *Why:* Proper identification and sequential navigation through these landmarks ensure safe access to the fourth ventricle while minimizing damage to surrounding structures.

- ⚠ The lower cranial nerves IX, X, and XII must be preserved during the resection of a fourth-ventricle medulloblastoma to maintain swallow and airway function.
  - *Why:* Damage to these nerves can result in significant postoperative complications such as dysphagia and airway compromise, requiring prolonged rehabilitation or interventions.

- ⚠ Preserve the PICA and its perforators to the brainstem during the resection of a fourth-ventricle medulloblastoma.
  - *Why:* Injury to the PICA or its perforators can lead to ischemic complications in the brainstem and cerebellum, resulting in severe neurological deficits.

- ⚠ The facial colliculus on the floor of the fourth ventricle must be identified and preserved during tumor resection.
  - *Why:* The facial colliculus is crucial for facial nerve function; damage can lead to facial paralysis, impacting the patient's quality of life.

- ⚠ Anatomical variants in the course of the PICA should be anticipated and identified during surgery.
  - *Why:* Recognizing anatomical variants of the PICA can prevent inadvertent injury, which could lead to catastrophic ischemic events.

- ⚠ The 'no-fly zone' is the floor of the fourth ventricle itself, particularly the median sulcus and the regions of the facial colliculi and vagal trigones, where cranial nerve nuclei are located.
  - *Why:* Vague warnings are insufficient. This defines the precise anatomical areas where any surgical manipulation risks permanent, devastating injury to cranial nerve function.

- ⚠ The facial colliculus is a bulge on the dorsal pons, forming the medial eminence on the floor of the fourth ventricle, located superior to the striae medullares and medial to the sulcus limitans.
  - *Why:* Precise localization is critical. It contains the abducens nucleus (CN VI) and the motor fibers of the facial nerve (CN VII), making it an absolute no-fly zone to prevent facial paralysis and lateral gaze palsy.

## Operative Plan
*Critical steps, decision points, and stop criteria.*

- ⚠ The patient should be positioned prone with careful attention to head positioning to minimize the risk of venous air embolism during suboccipital craniotomy.
  - *Why:* Prone positioning optimizes surgical access but increases the risk of venous air embolism, which can be life-threatening if not properly managed.

- ⚠ Exposure involves a midline suboccipital craniotomy extending to the foramen magnum for access to the fourth ventricle.
  - *Why:* Adequate exposure is essential for safe and effective tumor resection while minimizing the risk of injury to surrounding structures.

- ⚠ Critical steps include opening the cisterna magna, identifying the cerebellar tonsils, and accessing the fourth ventricle via the telovelar approach.
  - *Why:* Following these critical steps ensures safe access to the tumor while preserving vital structures and minimizing complications.

- ⚠ Decide intraoperatively whether gross total resection is achievable without compromising critical structures.
  - *Why:* Balancing the extent of resection with the preservation of neurological function is crucial for optimal patient outcomes.

- ⚠ Stop resection if there is significant bleeding from the PICA or its perforators that cannot be controlled.
  - *Why:* Uncontrolled bleeding can lead to catastrophic outcomes, including ischemia and brainstem injury, necessitating a halt in the procedure.

- ⚠ Achieve a watertight dural closure using a graft and sealant to prevent pseudomeningocele formation.
  - *Why:* A watertight closure is essential to prevent CSF leaks and subsequent complications such as pseudomeningocele and infection.

- ⚠ Intraoperative neuromonitoring must include BAERs (auditory pathway), somatosensory evoked potentials (dorsal columns), MEPs (corticospinal tracts), and EMG for cranial nerves V, VII, IX, X, XI, and XII.
  - *Why:* Comprehensive monitoring provides real-time feedback on the functional integrity of the brainstem, corticospinal tracts, and specific lower cranial nerves at risk during dissection, allowing for immediate surgical adjustment to prevent permanent deficits.

- ⚠ Use neuronavigation to accurately localize the tumor and critical structures during resection.
  - *Why:* Neuronavigation enhances surgical precision, reducing the risk of injury to vital structures and improving resection outcomes.

- ⚠ Confirm the attending's preference for post-operative CSF diversion strategy, such as EVD or VP shunt, based on intraoperative findings.
  - *Why:* Proper management of postoperative hydrocephalus is critical to prevent increased intracranial pressure and associated complications.

- ⚠ Anesthesia brief: Confirm Type & Cross for at least 2 units pRBCs, availability of mannitol/Decadron, and placement of precordial Doppler and central line for VAE monitoring/rescue.
  - *Why:* Ensures the entire team is prepared for expected challenges (blood loss) and potential catastrophes (VAE, brain swelling) specific to pediatric posterior fossa surgery.

- ⚠ Pediatric thermoregulation is maintained with a forced-air warming blanket and warmed IV fluids; use a reinforced/armored endotracheal tube to prevent kinking in the prone position.
  - *Why:* Children have a high surface-area-to-volume ratio, making them prone to hypothermia, which can cause coagulopathy and delayed emergence. Prone positioning risks airway compromise if the ETT is not secure.

- ⚠ Tumor specimens must be sent fresh/unfixed for molecular subgrouping (WNT, SHH, Group 3, Group 4) and separately in formalin for standard histopathology.
  - *Why:* Molecular subgrouping is the standard of care for medulloblastoma and dictates post-operative risk stratification and adjuvant therapy (radiation/chemotherapy), directly impacting survival and long-term morbidity.

- ⚠ Oncologic staging plan: Post-op brain MRI within 48h for residual tumor, followed by full neuraxis (spine) MRI and CSF cytology (from EVD or LP) within 2 weeks to check for leptomeningeal dissemination (M-stage).
  - *Why:* Complete staging is required to determine the patient's risk group (standard vs. high-risk) and guide the intensity of adjuvant therapy according to established treatment protocols (e.g., COG).

## Risk and Rescue
*Expected and catastrophic complications with rescue sequences.*

- ⚠ Cerebellar mutism syndrome is a likely complication following resection of a fourth-ventricle medulloblastoma.
  - *Why:* Cerebellar mutism can significantly impact the patient's recovery and quality of life, necessitating early intervention and rehabilitation.

- ⚠ Injury to the PICA can lead to catastrophic ischemic events in the cerebellum and brainstem.
  - *Why:* PICA injury can result in severe neurological deficits, requiring immediate recognition and management to prevent permanent damage.

- ⚠ Preoperative placement of an EVD or ETV can mitigate the risk of hydrocephalus exacerbation during surgery.
  - *Why:* Managing hydrocephalus preoperatively reduces intraoperative brain swelling and facilitates safer surgical resection.

- ⚠ In the event of a brainstem hemorrhage, immediately control bleeding, stabilize the patient, and consider intraoperative imaging to assess damage.
  - *Why:* Rapid response to brainstem hemorrhage is critical to prevent severe neurological impairment or death.

- ⚠ Brainstem injury during tumor resection can result in severe neurological deficits or death.
  - *Why:* Avoiding brainstem injury is paramount, as it can lead to irreversible damage and significantly impact the patient's survival and quality of life.

- ⚠ Rescue Triggers
  - *Why:* This provides a clear, sequential, and multi-disciplinary rescue protocol. The Trendelenburg position is critical to trap air in the right atrium, preventing its entry into the pulmonary circulation (air lock).
  - [ ] Rescue for venous air embolism: 1.
  - [ ] Anesthesiologist notifies surgeon.
  - [ ] Surgeon floods the field with saline and applies bone wax to bone edges.
  - [ ] Anesthesiologist places patient in Trendelenburg (head-down) position and aspirates air from the right atrial/central venous catheter.
  - [ ] Administer 100% FiO2 and vasopressors as needed.

- ⚠ Postoperative pseudomeningocele formation is a likely complication if dural closure is not watertight.
  - *Why:* Pseudomeningocele can lead to CSF leaks and infection, requiring additional interventions and prolonging recovery.

- ⚠ Administer seizure prophylaxis postoperatively to reduce the risk of seizures following fourth-ventricle tumor resection.
  - *Why:* Seizures can complicate recovery and increase the risk of further neurological injury, making prophylaxis an important consideration.

- ⚠ Postoperative hydrocephalus is a common complication following fourth-ventricle tumor resection.
  - *Why:* Hydrocephalus can lead to increased intracranial pressure and neurological deterioration, requiring prompt management.

- ⚠ Plan for early postoperative imaging, including MRI within 48 hours, to assess for residual tumor and complications.
  - *Why:* Early imaging helps identify residual disease and complications, guiding further treatment and improving outcomes.

- ⚠ If postoperative bulbar dysfunction is noted, initiate swallow evaluation and consider temporary nutritional support.
  - *Why:* Early identification and management of bulbar dysfunction prevent aspiration and ensure adequate nutrition during recovery.

- ⚠ Calculate Allowable Blood Loss (ABL) pre-operatively (e.g., for a 20kg child, EBV ~80ml/kg = 1600ml; ABL for Hct drop from 36 to 25 is ~370ml). Transfusion trigger is typically set at 15-20% of EBV loss or Hct < 25%.
  - *Why:* Pediatric patients have low total blood volume, making even small losses hemodynamically significant. A pre-calculated ABL and clear transfusion trigger are essential for proactive resuscitation.

- ⚠ Rescue Triggers
  - *Why:* Provides a structured, step-wise algorithm for managing a catastrophic complication, maximizing the chance of vessel preservation and minimizing ischemic damage to the brainstem and cerebellum.
  - [ ] Rescue for PICA injury: 1.
  - [ ] Proximal control with temporary clip.
  - [ ] Gentle suction on the vessel to identify the injury site.
  - [ ] Repair with 9-0 or 10-0 suture if a simple tear.
  - [ ] If unreparable, consider sacrifice vs.
  - [ ] bypass (e.g., occipital artery to PICA) based on ICG/flow assessment and patient stability.
  - [ ] Confirm flow post-repair.

- ⚠ Rescue Triggers
  - *Why:* Aggressive attempts to stop brainstem bleeding with cautery can cause devastating thermal injury to critical nuclei. This rescue sequence prioritizes patient safety over extent of resection.
  - [ ] Rescue for brainstem hemorrhage: 1.
  - [ ] Immediately apply gentle pressure with a cottonoid patty.
  - [ ] Apply hemostatic agents (e.g., Floseal, Surgiflo).
  - [ ] Avoid bipolar cautery directly on the brainstem floor.
  - [ ] If bleeding persists, leave a hemostatic agent in place and accept a subtotal resection.

- ⚠ Mitigation
  - *Why:* Up to 40% of patients require permanent CSF diversion. A clear weaning protocol prevents premature shunting while ensuring timely intervention for persistent hydrocephalus.
  - [ ] Post-op CSF diversion plan: If an EVD is placed, attempt to wean by clamping for 24h and raising the level by 5cm daily.
  - [ ] Shunt dependency is determined by clinical signs of ICP, radiographic ventriculomegaly, or failure to wean.
  - [ ] A VP shunt is the definitive treatment if needed.

- ⚠ PICU Handover: Specify monitoring for posterior fossa syndrome (mutism, ataxia, emotional lability), bulbar dysfunction (cough/gag reflex, pooling secretions), level of consciousness, and signs of hydrocephalus. Bailout: Any acute decline in GCS or new focal deficit triggers a STAT non-contrast head CT.
  - *Why:* Provides the receiving ICU team with specific, high-yield parameters to monitor and clear action triggers, enabling early detection and management of life-threatening post-operative complications like hemorrhage or acute hydrocephalus.
