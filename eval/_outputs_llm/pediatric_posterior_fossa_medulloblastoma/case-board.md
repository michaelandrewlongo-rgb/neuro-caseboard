## Anatomy at Risk
*Structures to identify, preserve, or monitor for this approach.*

- ⚠ The surgical corridor for a suboccipital craniotomy involves accessing the fourth ventricle through the cerebellar vermis or cerebellar tonsils.
  - *Why:* Understanding the surgical corridor is crucial to minimize damage to surrounding cerebellar structures and ensure safe access to the tumor.

- ⚠ Key landmarks include the torcula, transverse sinus, and the foramen of Magendie.
  - *Why:* Identifying these landmarks helps in orienting the surgeon during the procedure and avoiding critical vascular structures.

- ⚠ The cerebellar tonsils and vermis are critical neural structures at risk during a suboccipital craniotomy.
  - *Why:* Preserving these structures is essential to maintain cerebellar function and prevent ataxia.

- ⚠ The posterior inferior cerebellar artery (PICA) is at risk during the approach to the fourth ventricle.
  - *Why:* Injury to PICA can lead to cerebellar infarction and significant neurological deficits.

- ⚠ The floor of the fourth ventricle contains cranial nerve nuclei, including the vagal and hypoglossal nuclei.
  - *Why:* Damage to these nuclei can result in dysphagia and tongue weakness, impacting the patient's quality of life.

- ⚠ Anatomic variants such as a low-lying torcula or high-riding transverse sinus can alter the approach.
  - *Why:* Recognizing these variants preoperatively can help in planning the surgical approach and avoiding venous injury.

- ⚠ The floor of the fourth ventricle is a no-fly zone due to the presence of critical cranial nerve nuclei.
  - *Why:* Avoiding this area is essential to prevent severe neurological deficits postoperatively.

## Operative Plan
*Critical steps, decision points, and stop criteria.*

- ⚠ The patient is positioned prone with the head flexed to optimize access to the suboccipital region.
  - *Why:* Proper positioning is crucial to provide adequate exposure and minimize venous congestion.

- ⚠ Exposure involves a midline suboccipital incision extending from the inion to C2.
  - *Why:* This exposure provides access to the posterior fossa and the fourth ventricle.

- ⚠ Critical steps include craniotomy, dural opening, and careful dissection of the cerebellar tonsils to access the fourth ventricle.
  - *Why:* Following these steps meticulously ensures safe tumor resection while preserving surrounding structures.

- ⚠ Decision points include whether to split the vermis or retract the tonsils for optimal tumor access.
  - *Why:* These decisions impact the extent of resection and potential postoperative cerebellar dysfunction.

- ⚠ Stop criteria include achieving gross total resection while preserving neurological function.
  - *Why:* Balancing tumor resection with functional preservation is key to optimal outcomes.

- ⚠ Closure involves watertight dural closure and replacement of the bone flap.
  - *Why:* Proper closure prevents cerebrospinal fluid leaks and potential infections.

- ⚠ Intraoperative neurophysiological monitoring of brainstem auditory evoked potentials is used.
  - *Why:* Monitoring helps detect and prevent injury to brainstem structures during surgery.

- ⚠ Use of an ultrasonic aspirator can facilitate tumor debulking.
  - *Why:* This tool aids in efficient and safe resection of the tumor mass.

## Risk and Rescue
*Expected and catastrophic complications with rescue sequences.*

- ⚠ Cerebellar mutism syndrome is a likely complication following resection.
  - *Why:* Awareness of this complication allows for early intervention and management.

- ⚠ Injury to the brainstem can result in catastrophic neurological deficits.
  - *Why:* Preventing brainstem injury is critical to avoid life-threatening complications.

- ⚠ Mitigation strategies include careful dissection and use of intraoperative monitoring.
  - *Why:* These strategies help minimize the risk of neurological injury during the procedure.

- ⚠ Rescue triggers include sudden changes in evoked potentials indicating potential brainstem compromise.
  - *Why:* Immediate recognition and response to these triggers can prevent permanent neurological damage.
