## Anatomy at Risk
*Structures to identify, preserve, or monitor for this approach.*

- ⚠ The surgical corridor for a suboccipital craniotomy involves accessing the posterior fossa through the occipital bone, requiring careful dissection to avoid injury to the cerebellar hemispheres.
  - *Why:* Precise navigation through the posterior fossa is crucial to avoid damaging the cerebellum and brainstem, which are vital for motor coordination and autonomic functions.

- ⚠ Key landmarks in order for this procedure include the occipital bone, foramen magnum, cerebellar hemispheres, and fourth ventricle.
  - *Why:* Identifying these landmarks ensures accurate localization of the tumor and minimizes the risk of injury to surrounding structures.

- ⚠ The fourth ventricle is bordered by the cerebellum and brainstem, with the floor formed by the rhomboid fossa.
  - *Why:* Understanding the anatomy of the fourth ventricle is essential to avoid damaging the brainstem during tumor resection.

- ⚠ The vertebral arteries run along the lateral aspect of the medulla and can be at risk during exposure of the fourth ventricle.
  - *Why:* Injury to the vertebral arteries can lead to significant bleeding and ischemic complications.

- ⚠ The floor of the fourth ventricle contains nuclei critical for autonomic functions, including the vagal and hypoglossal nuclei.
  - *Why:* Damage to these nuclei can result in severe autonomic dysfunction and cranial nerve deficits.

- ⚠ Anatomic variants such as a low-lying cerebellar tonsil or Chiari malformation may alter the surgical approach.
  - *Why:* Recognizing these variants is crucial for adapting the surgical plan to ensure safe access and resection.

- ⚠ Avoid the brainstem and cranial nerve nuclei located in the floor of the fourth ventricle during tumor resection.
  - *Why:* Injury to these areas can result in irreversible neurological deficits and life-threatening complications.

- ⚠ Which specific veins and sinuses must be preserved during suboccipital craniotomy for fourth-ventricle medulloblastoma resection?
  - *Why:* Preservation of specific veins and sinuses, such as the transverse and sigmoid sinuses, is crucial to prevent venous congestion and hemorrhagic complications during surgery.

## Operative Plan
*Critical steps, decision points, and stop criteria.*

- ⚠ Position the patient prone with the head flexed to optimize access to the posterior fossa and minimize venous congestion.
  - *Why:* Proper positioning is essential for surgical exposure and reducing the risk of complications such as air embolism.

- ⚠ Perform a midline suboccipital incision and craniotomy to expose the cerebellar hemispheres and fourth ventricle.
  - *Why:* Adequate exposure is necessary to visualize and access the tumor while preserving surrounding neural structures.

- ⚠ Critical steps include dural opening, cerebellar retraction, tumor identification, and resection using ultrasonic aspirator.
  - *Why:* Following these steps ensures systematic and safe removal of the tumor while minimizing damage to adjacent tissues.

- ⚠ Decision points include assessing tumor resectability and determining the need for adjunctive therapies such as chemotherapy.
  - *Why:* These decisions impact the extent of resection and postoperative management, influencing patient outcomes.

- ⚠ Stop resection if there is excessive bleeding or if critical structures such as the brainstem are at risk.
  - *Why:* Identifying stop points prevents catastrophic complications and preserves neurological function.

- ⚠ Closure involves meticulous dural repair and cranioplasty to prevent CSF leak and ensure structural integrity.
  - *Why:* Proper closure is essential to prevent postoperative complications such as CSF leak and infection.

- ⚠ Intraoperative monitoring includes facial nerve EMG and brainstem auditory evoked potentials (BAEPs) to assess neural function.
  - *Why:* Monitoring helps detect early signs of neural compromise, allowing for immediate intervention to prevent permanent damage.

- ⚠ Utilize neuronavigation and intraoperative ultrasound to enhance tumor localization and resection accuracy.
  - *Why:* These adjuncts improve surgical precision and reduce the risk of residual tumor and damage to critical structures.

- ⚠ Preference for using dexamethasone to reduce peritumoral edema and improve surgical field visibility.
  - *Why:* Dexamethasone helps manage edema, facilitating safer resection and reducing postoperative complications.

- ⚠ What are the named feeding vessels of the medulloblastoma that may require embolization prior to resection?
  - *Why:* Identifying and potentially embolizing feeding vessels such as branches of the posterior inferior cerebellar artery (PICA) can minimize intraoperative bleeding and facilitate safer tumor resection.

- ⚠ What is the role of ICG videoangiography in this procedure?
  - *Why:* ICG videoangiography can be used intraoperatively to assess blood flow and ensure preservation of critical vascular structures, reducing the risk of ischemic complications.

## Risk and Rescue
*Expected and catastrophic complications with rescue sequences.*

- ⚠ Common complications include CSF leak, cerebellar mutism, and cranial nerve deficits.
  - *Why:* Anticipating these complications allows for proactive measures to minimize their occurrence and impact.

- ⚠ Catastrophic complications include brainstem injury leading to respiratory failure.
  - *Why:* Brainstem injury can be life-threatening, requiring immediate intervention to secure the airway and stabilize the patient.

- ⚠ Massive hemorrhage from vertebral artery injury can lead to hemodynamic instability.
  - *Why:* Rapid control of bleeding and restoration of hemodynamic stability are critical to prevent morbidity and mortality.

- ⚠ Mitigation strategies include meticulous surgical technique and intraoperative monitoring to avoid injury to critical structures.
  - *Why:* Preventive measures reduce the risk of complications and improve surgical outcomes.

- ⚠ Rescue triggers for brainstem injury include sudden loss of BAEPs and facial nerve EMG changes.
  - *Why:* Early detection of neural compromise allows for immediate corrective actions to prevent permanent deficits.

- ⚠ Rescue sequence for vertebral artery injury includes packing with hemostatic agents, vessel repair, and transfusion support.
  - *Why:* Effective management of vascular injury is crucial to prevent exsanguination and stabilize the patient.

- ⚠ Rescue sequence for CSF leak involves dural patching and lumbar drain placement to divert CSF and promote healing.
  - *Why:* Addressing CSF leaks promptly prevents complications such as infection and pseudomeningocele formation.

- ⚠ What is the step-by-step rescue sequence for catastrophic brainstem injury during this procedure?
  - *Why:* A detailed rescue sequence for brainstem injury, including immediate cessation of resection, securing airway, and stabilizing hemodynamics, is essential to manage this life-threatening complication effectively.
