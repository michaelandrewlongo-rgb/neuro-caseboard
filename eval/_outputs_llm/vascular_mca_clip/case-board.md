## Anatomy at Risk
*Structures to identify, preserve, or monitor for this approach.*

- ⚠ The pterional craniotomy involves a skin incision starting 1 cm anterior to the tragus and extending superiorly along the hairline to the midline.
  - *Why:* This incision provides optimal exposure to the sphenoid ridge and the Sylvian fissure, facilitating access to the MCA bifurcation.

- ⚠ Key landmarks include the Sylvian fissure, the sphenoid ridge, and the MCA bifurcation.
  - *Why:* These landmarks guide the surgeon to the aneurysm while minimizing brain retraction and potential injury.

- ⚠ The optic nerve runs medial to the surgical corridor and must be protected during dissection.
  - *Why:* Injury to the optic nerve can result in visual field deficits or blindness.

- ⚠ The anterior choroidal artery arises near the MCA bifurcation and must be preserved during aneurysm clipping.
  - *Why:* Occlusion of the anterior choroidal artery can lead to hemiplegia, hemianesthesia, and homonymous hemianopia.

- ⚠ The Broca's area is located in the inferior frontal gyrus, which is in proximity to the surgical field.
  - *Why:* Damage to Broca's area can result in expressive aphasia, impacting the patient's ability to speak.

- ⚠ Anatomic variants such as an accessory MCA or early bifurcation can alter the surgical approach.
  - *Why:* Awareness of these variants is crucial to avoid misidentifying the aneurysm or critical vessels.

- ⚠ The carotid artery and its bifurcation should be avoided during dissection to prevent catastrophic bleeding.
  - *Why:* Injury to the carotid artery can result in massive hemorrhage and stroke.

- ⚠ Which specific perforators from the MCA must be preserved during clipping to prevent ischemic complications?
  - *Why:* Preservation of MCA perforators is crucial to avoid ischemia in regions supplied by these vessels, particularly the basal ganglia and internal capsule.

## Operative Plan
*Critical steps, decision points, and stop criteria.*

- ⚠ The patient is positioned supine with the head turned 30 degrees to the right and slightly extended.
  - *Why:* This position optimizes access to the left Sylvian fissure and MCA bifurcation while minimizing brain retraction.

- ⚠ A frontotemporal craniotomy is performed, followed by opening of the dura in a curvilinear fashion.
  - *Why:* Adequate exposure is necessary to visualize the Sylvian fissure and MCA bifurcation for safe aneurysm clipping.

- ⚠ Dissect the Sylvian fissure to expose the MCA bifurcation and identify the aneurysm neck.
  - *Why:* Proper dissection of the Sylvian fissure is critical to access the aneurysm without damaging surrounding structures.

- ⚠ Decide whether temporary clipping of the proximal MCA is necessary to reduce aneurysm tension.
  - *Why:* Temporary clipping can facilitate safe aneurysm manipulation but carries a risk of ischemia.

- ⚠ Stop dissection if significant brain swelling occurs despite mannitol administration.
  - *Why:* Brain swelling can increase intracranial pressure and risk herniation, requiring immediate intervention.

- ⚠ Close the dura with a watertight suture and replace the bone flap, securing it with titanium plates.
  - *Why:* Proper closure prevents CSF leaks and ensures structural integrity of the skull.

- ⚠ Use somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEPs) to monitor for ischemic changes during clipping.
  - *Why:* SSEPs provide real-time feedback on neural function, allowing for immediate intervention if changes occur.

- ⚠ Utilize intraoperative Doppler ultrasound to confirm vessel patency after clipping.
  - *Why:* Doppler ultrasound ensures that blood flow is maintained in the MCA and its branches post-clipping.

- ⚠ The attending prefers using a Sugita clip for the aneurysm due to its strong closing force.
  - *Why:* The choice of clip can affect the security of the aneurysm occlusion and risk of recurrence.

- ⚠ What role does indocyanine green (ICG) videoangiography play in confirming aneurysm exclusion and vessel patency?
  - *Why:* ICG videoangiography provides real-time visualization of blood flow, ensuring that the aneurysm is successfully excluded and that parent and perforating vessels remain patent.

- ⚠ What specific steps should be taken to manage brain relaxation during exposure, particularly if initial measures are insufficient?
  - *Why:* Effective brain relaxation is essential for safe dissection and clipping. If initial measures like mannitol and hyperventilation are insufficient, additional strategies such as CSF drainage may be necessary.

## Risk and Rescue
*Expected and catastrophic complications with rescue sequences.*

- ⚠ Vasospasm is a common complication following subarachnoid hemorrhage and can lead to ischemic deficits.
  - *Why:* Early detection and treatment with nimodipine and hypertensive therapy can mitigate the risk of vasospasm.

- ⚠ CSF leak can occur if the dura is not closed properly, leading to potential infection.
  - *Why:* Ensuring a watertight dural closure is critical to prevent CSF leaks and subsequent complications.

- ⚠ Seizures may occur post-operatively due to cortical irritation.
  - *Why:* Prophylactic antiepileptic drugs may be administered to reduce the risk of seizures.

- ⚠ Infection is a risk with any craniotomy and can lead to meningitis or abscess formation.
  - *Why:* Strict sterile technique and perioperative antibiotics are essential to minimize infection risk.

- ⚠ Intraoperative rupture of the aneurysm can lead to massive hemorrhage.
  - *Why:* Immediate control with suction, temporary clipping of the parent vessel, and rapid aneurysm clipping is necessary to manage rupture.

- ⚠ Occlusion of a major artery during clipping can cause a stroke.
  - *Why:* If occlusion is suspected, remove the clip and reassess the aneurysm neck and vessel anatomy before reapplying.

- ⚠ Severe brain swelling can lead to herniation.
  - *Why:* Administer hyperosmolar therapy, elevate the head, and consider decompressive craniectomy if swelling is uncontrollable.

- ⚠ Use of mannitol and hyperventilation can help reduce brain swelling intraoperatively.
  - *Why:* These measures decrease intracranial pressure and improve surgical access.

- ⚠ Intraoperative neurophysiological monitoring can detect early signs of ischemia.
  - *Why:* Early detection allows for immediate intervention to restore blood flow and prevent permanent deficits.

- ⚠ If significant changes in SSEPs are noted, consider repositioning the clip or removing it to restore blood flow.
  - *Why:* Timely intervention can prevent irreversible ischemic damage.

- ⚠ In the event of an intraoperative aneurysm rupture, immediately apply temporary clips to control bleeding.
  - *Why:* Rapid control of bleeding is essential to prevent catastrophic blood loss and maintain cerebral perfusion.

- ⚠ What is the step-by-step protocol for managing severe vasospasm intraoperatively?
  - *Why:* Severe vasospasm can lead to critical ischemia. A clear protocol, including the use of intra-arterial vasodilators or angioplasty, is necessary to promptly address this complication.

- ⚠ What is the step-by-step rescue protocol for severe brain swelling leading to herniation?
  - *Why:* Severe brain swelling can cause herniation, a life-threatening condition. A step-by-step rescue protocol, including measures like decompressive craniectomy, is necessary to manage this complication effectively.
