## Anatomy at Risk
*Structures to identify, preserve, or monitor for this approach.*

- ⚠ The surgical corridor for a pterional craniotomy involves splitting the Sylvian fissure to access the left MCA bifurcation aneurysm.
  - *Why:* Properly splitting the Sylvian fissure is crucial to provide adequate exposure of the aneurysm while minimizing retraction on the frontal and temporal lobes, reducing the risk of cortical injury.

- ⚠ The optic nerve and carotid artery are key landmarks at the skull base during a pterional craniotomy.
  - *Why:* Identifying these landmarks helps in orienting the surgeon and avoiding injury to critical structures during the approach to the aneurysm.

- ⚠ The optic nerve and chiasm are in close proximity to the surgical field during a pterional craniotomy for MCA aneurysm clipping.
  - *Why:* Awareness of the optic nerve and chiasm location is essential to prevent visual deficits from surgical manipulation or retraction.

- ⚠ Preservation of the lenticulostriate arteries is critical during the dissection of the MCA aneurysm.
  - *Why:* The lenticulostriate arteries supply deep brain structures, and their injury can lead to significant neurological deficits such as hemiparesis.

- ⚠ The Sylvian fissure dissection should protect the frontal and temporal lobes to prevent cortical damage.
  - *Why:* Protecting these lobes is vital to maintain cognitive and motor functions post-operatively.

- ⚠ Anatomical variants such as an accessory MCA or duplicated MCA should be considered during the approach.
  - *Why:* Recognizing these variants can alter the surgical strategy and prevent inadvertent vessel injury.

- ⚠ The optic nerve and chiasm are considered no-fly zones during the dissection to avoid visual complications.
  - *Why:* Avoiding these areas reduces the risk of postoperative visual deficits.

## Operative Plan
*Critical steps, decision points, and stop criteria.*

- ⚠ The patient is positioned supine with the head pinned and turned to the right for optimal exposure of the left MCA aneurysm.
  - *Why:* Proper positioning is crucial to maximize surgical exposure while minimizing venous congestion and risk of air embolism.

- ⚠ A pterional craniotomy with sphenoid ridge drilling is performed to expose the left MCA bifurcation aneurysm.
  - *Why:* This exposure provides a direct approach to the aneurysm while allowing for proximal control of the parent vessels.

- ⚠ Proximal control of the M1 segment is established before dissecting the aneurysm neck and dome.
  - *Why:* Having proximal control is essential to manage intraoperative rupture effectively and ensure safe aneurysm clipping.

- ⚠ The choice of clip (straight, curved, fenestrated) is made based on the aneurysm morphology and neck configuration.
  - *Why:* Selecting the appropriate clip ensures complete aneurysm obliteration while preserving parent vessel patency.

- ⚠ The procedure is halted if there is uncontrollable bleeding despite proximal control and temporary clipping.
  - *Why:* Recognizing when to stop can prevent further complications and allows for reassessment and stabilization of the patient.

- ⚠ Meticulous dural closure and hemostasis are performed to prevent postoperative CSF leaks and hematoma formation.
  - *Why:* Proper closure reduces the risk of infection and ensures a stable postoperative course.

- ⚠ ICG videoangiography and micro-Doppler are used to confirm clip position and vessel patency intraoperatively.
  - *Why:* These tools help verify that the aneurysm is completely clipped and that blood flow is preserved in the parent vessels.

- ⚠ An external ventricular drain (EVD) is placed for acute hydrocephalus management post-clipping.
  - *Why:* An EVD allows for CSF drainage, reducing intracranial pressure and managing hydrocephalus, which is common after SAH.

- ⚠ Verify the attending's preference for adenosine-induced flow arrest versus rapid pacing in case of intraoperative rupture.
  - *Why:* Understanding the attending's preferred rescue technique ensures preparedness and swift action during an emergency.

## Risk and Rescue
*Expected and catastrophic complications with rescue sequences.*

- ⚠ Vasospasm is a likely complication following SAH and requires nimodipine for prophylaxis.
  - *Why:* Vasospasm can lead to delayed cerebral ischemia, so prophylactic treatment is crucial to prevent secondary injury.

- ⚠ Intraoperative aneurysm rupture is a catastrophic complication that requires immediate proximal control, suction, and neck clipping.
  - *Why:* Quick and effective management of rupture is critical to prevent severe hemorrhage and maintain patient stability.

- ⚠ Pre-clipping blood pressure control with a target SBP <140 mmHg reduces the risk of aneurysm re-rupture.
  - *Why:* Maintaining controlled blood pressure minimizes the risk of intraoperative rupture and associated complications.

- ⚠ Adenosine-induced flow arrest is triggered if temporary clipping and suction fail to control an intraoperative rupture.
  - *Why:* Adenosine provides a brief period of cardiac standstill, allowing for controlled clipping of the aneurysm neck.

- ⚠ Seizures are a potential complication post-operatively, and prophylaxis with Keppra is recommended.
  - *Why:* Seizure prophylaxis reduces the risk of postoperative seizures, which can complicate recovery and increase morbidity.

- ⚠ Hydrocephalus is a common complication after SAH, managed with an external ventricular drain (EVD).
  - *Why:* Timely management of hydrocephalus is essential to prevent increased intracranial pressure and associated neurological decline.

- ⚠ Re-rupture precautions include strict blood pressure control before securing the aneurysm.
  - *Why:* Preventing re-rupture is vital to avoid additional hemorrhagic events and worsening of the patient's condition.

- ⚠ Catastrophic vasospasm post-operatively requires aggressive management with hypertensive therapy and nimodipine.
  - *Why:* Severe vasospasm can lead to ischemic strokes, necessitating prompt intervention to restore cerebral perfusion.

- ⚠ Acute subdural hematoma formation post-operatively requires immediate surgical evacuation.
  - *Why:* Rapid intervention is necessary to relieve pressure on the brain and prevent irreversible damage.

- ⚠ Immediate post-operative angiogram is performed to confirm aneurysm obliteration and assess for any complications.
  - *Why:* Early detection of complications allows for timely intervention and improves patient outcomes.
