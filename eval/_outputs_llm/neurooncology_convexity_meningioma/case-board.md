## Anatomy at Risk
*Structures to identify, preserve, or monitor for this approach.*

- ⚠ The surgical corridor for a right frontal convexity meningioma resection involves a right frontal craniotomy, avoiding eloquent cortex and preserving cortical draining veins.
  - *Why:* Establishing a clear surgical corridor is crucial to minimize damage to surrounding brain tissue and preserve important structures such as cortical veins.

- ⚠ Key landmarks in order for this procedure include the coronal suture, superior sagittal sinus, and frontal lobe surface.
  - *Why:* Identifying these landmarks ensures precise orientation and helps avoid damage to critical structures like the superior sagittal sinus.

- ⚠ The right precentral gyrus is in proximity to the surgical field and should be identified and preserved to avoid motor deficits.
  - *Why:* Avoiding damage to the precentral gyrus is essential to prevent postoperative motor deficits.

- ⚠ The superior sagittal sinus and cortical bridging veins must be preserved during the procedure to prevent venous infarction and hemorrhage.
  - *Why:* Preservation of venous structures is crucial to maintaining normal venous drainage and avoiding complications such as venous infarction.

- ⚠ The supplementary motor area (SMA) is near the surgical site and should be avoided to prevent SMA syndrome, characterized by transient motor deficits.
  - *Why:* Understanding the proximity of the SMA helps in planning the surgical approach to minimize the risk of postoperative motor deficits.

- ⚠ Anatomical variants such as variations in the course of the middle meningeal artery should be anticipated and identified during surgery.
  - *Why:* Awareness of anatomical variants helps in planning the surgical approach and avoiding unexpected bleeding or complications.

- ⚠ The superior sagittal sinus and cortical bridging veins are no-fly zones during this procedure to prevent catastrophic bleeding and venous infarction.
  - *Why:* Identifying no-fly zones is critical to avoid intraoperative complications that could lead to significant morbidity.

- ⚠ Subcortical structures at risk include the corticospinal tracts (CST) deep to the motor cortex and the superior longitudinal fasciculus (SLF) in the frontal white matter.
  - *Why:* Injury to these white matter tracts can cause permanent motor or cognitive deficits. Subcortical stimulation mapping is used to maintain a safe margin from these tracts during deep dissection.

## Operative Plan
*Critical steps, decision points, and stop criteria.*

- ⚠ The patient is positioned supine with the head turned slightly to the left to facilitate access to the right frontal region while minimizing venous air embolism risk.
  - *Why:* Proper positioning is essential to optimize surgical access and minimize risks such as venous air embolism.

- ⚠ Exposure is achieved through a right frontal craniotomy designed to expose the tumor and a 1-2 cm margin of surrounding dura for circumferential devascularization and resection.
  - *Why:* A Simpson Grade I resection requires en bloc removal of the tumor and its dural attachment. The exposure must facilitate this, not preserve the involved dura.

- ⚠ Critical steps include circumferential dural devascularization, internal debulking of the tumor, and careful dissection off the pial plane.
  - *Why:* Following these critical steps ensures a systematic approach to tumor resection, minimizing complications and maximizing resection completeness.

- ⚠ Decision points include assessing the need for pre-operative embolization of the middle meningeal artery based on tumor vascularity.
  - *Why:* Deciding on pre-operative embolization can reduce intraoperative blood loss and facilitate safer tumor resection.

- ⚠ Stop criteria include achieving a Simpson Grade I resection, defined as complete removal of the tumor, its dural attachment, and any involved/hyperostotic underlying bone.
  - *Why:* Omitting resection of involved bone downgrades the resection to a Simpson II and increases the risk of recurrence.

- ⚠ Closure involves dural reconstruction using a pericranial graft and ensuring a watertight closure to prevent CSF leaks.
  - *Why:* Watertight closure is crucial to prevent postoperative CSF leaks and associated complications.

- ⚠ Intraoperative neuromonitoring includes motor mapping to avoid damage to the precentral gyrus and supplementary motor area.
  - *Why:* Neuromonitoring helps prevent postoperative neurological deficits by ensuring functional areas are preserved during resection.

- ⚠ The use of an ultrasonic aspirator facilitates internal debulking of the tumor, reducing mass effect and aiding in safe resection.
  - *Why:* The ultrasonic aspirator allows for efficient tumor debulking while minimizing damage to surrounding brain tissue.

- ⚠ Verify the attending's preference for the use of intraoperative dexamethasone to manage peritumoral edema and ensure gentle brain handling.
  - *Why:* Understanding the attending's preferences ensures the surgical team is aligned and prepared for intraoperative management strategies.

- ⚠ Anesthesia brief: Confirm 2 large-bore IVs are running, type and cross for 2-4 units pRBCs is complete, and mannitol (1 g/kg) or hypertonic saline is available for brain relaxation.
  - *Why:* Meningiomas can be highly vascular. This ensures the team is prepared for significant blood loss and has tools for managing brain swelling during dural opening and tumor manipulation.

- ⚠ Perioperative steroid regimen: Dexamethasone 10 mg IV pre-operatively, followed by a taper (e.g., 4 mg q6h post-op, weaned over 5-7 days) to manage peritumoral edema.
  - *Why:* Controls vasogenic edema, improves brain relaxation, and can mitigate post-operative neurological deficits. A clear taper plan prevents adrenal insufficiency.

- ⚠ The central sulcus is localized using somatosensory evoked potential (SSEP) phase reversal by stimulating the contralateral median nerve.
  - *Why:* Provides definitive, electrophysiological identification of the motor strip (precentral gyrus) vs. sensory strip (postcentral gyrus), establishing a key 'no-fly zone' before resection begins.

- ⚠ Cortical motor mapping is performed with a monopolar or bipolar stimulator (e.g., train-of-five at 50Hz), starting at 3-5 mA and increasing until a motor response is elicited or a ceiling of ~15-20 mA is reached.
  - *Why:* Establishes the functional boundary of the motor cortex. Subcortical mapping during resection uses lower thresholds (1-3 mA) to detect proximity to the corticospinal tracts.

- ⚠ Any hyperostotic or tumor-invaded bone identified on pre-op imaging or intra-operatively is drilled down or resected, followed by cranioplasty using titanium mesh or a custom implant.
  - *Why:* Resection of involved bone is required for a Simpson Grade I resection and to minimize recurrence. Cranioplasty restores cranial contour and provides protection.

- ⚠ Hemostasis strategy: Bipolar coagulation for dural feeders and pial vessels, bone wax for diploic bleeding, and topical hemostatics (e.g., thrombin-soaked Gelfoam, Floseal) for venous oozing and raw tumor bed.
  - *Why:* A multi-modal strategy is essential for managing different sources of bleeding (arterial, venous, bone) to maintain a clear field and minimize blood loss.

- ⚠ Immediate post-operative plan: Head CT in PACU to rule out hematoma, transfer to ICU/step-down for hourly neuro checks for 24 hours, and maintain SBP < 140 mmHg.
  - *Why:* Defines the critical handoff and monitoring plan to detect and manage immediate post-operative complications like hemorrhage or neurological decline.

## Risk and Rescue
*Expected and catastrophic complications with rescue sequences.*

- ⚠ Likely complications include CSF leak, infection, and venous infarction due to cortical vein injury.
  - *Why:* Awareness of likely complications allows for proactive measures to prevent and manage these issues, improving patient outcomes.

- ⚠ Catastrophic complications include massive bleeding from the superior sagittal sinus, which requires immediate packing and tamponade, followed by surgical repair.
  - *Why:* Recognizing and managing catastrophic complications promptly can be life-saving and prevent significant morbidity.

- ⚠ Mitigation strategies include pre-operative embolization of feeding vessels and careful intraoperative hemostasis to reduce blood loss.
  - *Why:* Effective mitigation strategies reduce the risk of intraoperative complications and improve surgical outcomes.

- ⚠ Rescue triggers for superior sagittal sinus injury include sudden massive bleeding, requiring immediate packing and surgical repair to control hemorrhage.
  - *Why:* Identifying rescue triggers allows for rapid intervention, minimizing the impact of catastrophic complications.

- ⚠ Seizure prophylaxis with levetiracetam (e.g., 1000 mg IV load pre-op, then 500-1000 mg BID) is continued for at least 7 days post-operatively.
  - *Why:* Supratentorial surgery and cortical manipulation carry a risk of seizures. Prophylaxis is standard practice to prevent post-operative seizures which can increase ICP and risk hematoma.

- ⚠ Intra-operative seizure rescue sequence: Announce 'seizure' to anesthesia, immediately irrigate the cortex with cold saline/slush, and have anesthesia administer a bolus of propofol or midazolam.
  - *Why:* Stimulation-induced seizures must be terminated immediately to prevent neuronal injury and brain swelling. This is a standardized, multi-personnel rapid response.

- ⚠ Catastrophic Complications
  - *Why:* Provides a step-by-step algorithm for managing one of the most feared complications, prioritizing hemorrhage control while attempting to preserve sinus patency.
  - [ ] SSS laceration rescue: 1.
  - [ ] Announce 'sinus bleed' and lower head of bed.
  - [ ] Apply direct pressure with a large cottonoid.
  - [ ] Obtain proximal/distal control if possible.
  - [ ] Repair with 5-0 or 6-0 Prolene suture.
  - [ ] If repair fails, use a patch (pericranium, muscle) or hemostatic agent (Surgicel/Floseal) to buttress the repair, avoiding sinus occlusion.

- ⚠ Pre-operative counseling for tumors near the midline includes the risk of transient Supplementary Motor Area (SMA) syndrome, characterized by contralateral akinesia and mutism that typically resolves over days to weeks.
  - *Why:* Manages patient and family expectations for a potentially alarming but usually temporary post-operative deficit, preventing panic and clarifying the expected clinical course.
